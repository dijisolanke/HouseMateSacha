Glass blowing have been traditionally used to produce items for use in our homes, whether one-off or mass-produced. By operating within this context, my new body of work has become a an introspection or subversion of our relationship to “functional” objects. I’d like to invite people to see everyday objects with surprise, as if it was the first time.

Through the medium of hot glass goblet-making, I am able to manipulate form easily and play with distortion, scale and balance. By doing so, I immediately challenge the functionality of the vessel, making it obsolete to the point of absurdity. I find this exploration of the absurd interesting, as with humour, it invites the viewer to contemplate the piece, and imagine another use or meaning for it.

Using the wine glass as a template, I also hope to address symbolic and socio-economic aspects within the work. Throughout history, quality hand-blown glasses have been a sign of wealth and social standing. By twisting them into impossible shapes that undermine the function, I also question their dignity and sophistication and ask what it would now mean to own one of these them.

Most importantly though, I see what I am doing as a search for poetry in everyday objects. My creations are in some way a reflection of my struggle to fit into the system and perhaps my wish to bend reality, in order to extract meaning out of it. I’d like to encourage people to dream in these times which can seem harshly judgmental and materialistic. Through their own quizzical and playful spirit, I hope these objects might do that
